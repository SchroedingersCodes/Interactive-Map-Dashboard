Niger State, located in North-Central Nigeria, is the largest state in the country by landmass, covering approximately 76,000 square kilometers. The state has an estimated population of over **5.7 million people** and is predominantly rural, with agriculture being the major occupation. Crops like rice, millet, maize, and yams thrive here, alongside cattle rearing, contributing to the state's economy. The state also possesses rich mineral resources, including gold, marble, and limestone.

Health centers in Niger state are predominantly categorized into primary, secondary and tertiary healthcare centers. However, despite its agricultural and natural wealth, Niger State faces significant challenges in healthcare delivery. The state has a high number of primary health centers (PHCs), especially in rural areas, but there is a severe shortage of secondary and tertiary healthcare facilities. This imbalance places immense pressure on primary centers, which are often ill-equipped to handle complex cases. People in need of specialized care frequently have to travel to neighboring states, contributing to healthcare inefficiencies and delays in treatment.

Healthcare Situation in Niger State: Niger State has a network of 1967 primary healthcare centers, but the number of secondary facilities is only around 32, and there are only 11 tertiary hospitals. This unequal distribution poses a major challenge for the healthcare system, especially for those in rural areas who rely on Primary Healthcare Centers for all forms of care, including emergencies.

Challenges:

  • Understaffing and Equipment Shortages: Many Primary Healthcare Centers lack qualified doctors and nurses, and the available medical equipment is often outdated or insufficient.
  • Rural Healthcare Access: People in remote parts of Niger State face long distances to access secondary or tertiary care, which often results in avoidable health complications.
  • Inadequate Referral Systems: The state has a poor referral system between Primary Healthcare Centers and higher-level facilities, leading to delays in receiving specialized treatment.

The public sector owns about 54% of healthcare centers in Niger state while the private sector accounts for approximately 2% while others such as religious and humanitarian organizations account for 44%
